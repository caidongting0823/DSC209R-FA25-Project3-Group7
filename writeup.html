Description

This interactive visualization explores one century of U.S. earthquakes (1925–2025) with a focus on moment magnitude (Mw ≥ 4.5), depth, and geography. Each earthquake is drawn as a mark on an Albers USA map with dedicated insets for Alaska, Hawaii, and Puerto Rico. We encode magnitude by point size (√-scaled so larger events stand out without overwhelming the view) and depth by a perceptual color ramp emphasizing shallow crustal differences. Users can filter by year, magnitude, and depth via dual-range sliders and quickly narrow the view to specific states or cities using a searchable location selector. Details-on-demand tooltips reveal normalized Mw values (when available) alongside the original reported scale, helping the audience compare events that were measured using different magnitude types.

Reflection

Our goal was to make earthquake risk and patterns tangible at the event level while remaining faithful to seismological nuance. Early pilots that mapped magnitude to color proved harder to read in dense clusters and made depth “invisible,” so we chose size→magnitude and color→depth as orthogonal encodings. To address the comparability issue across magnitude types (ML, Ms, mb, etc.), we surface Mw when reported or reliably approximated, mark non-comparable cases distinctly, and standardize labels in a unit-first style (e.g., “Mw 5.7”). Interaction is the primary vehicle for insight: users can slice the century by time, depth, magnitude, and place; story buttons highlight notable subsets (e.g., deepest, most recent), and the map supports smooth pan/zoom. Across iterations, we fixed label overflow, tuned radius limits, and refined the depth legend to 275 km to improve trust and legibility.
